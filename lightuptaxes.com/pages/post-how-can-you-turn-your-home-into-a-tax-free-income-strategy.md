What if your business could pay you—tax-free—just for using your home?

It sounds too good to be true, but it’s actually a legitimate tax strategy used by savvy business owners every year. It’s called the Augusta Rule (also known as IRS Section 280A(g)).

And if you’re not using it, you’re likely leaving money on the table.

What Is the Augusta Rule?

The Augusta Rule allows you to rent out your personal residence to your business for short periods of time—up to 14 days per year—and receive that rental income completely tax-free.

Yes, you read that right.

Your business pays you rent

You receive the income personally

You do not report it as taxable income

At the same time, your business gets a tax deduction for the rental expense.

It’s one of the rare strategies that creates a win-win on both sides.

How Business Owners Actually Use This

This strategy works best for business owners who can justify legitimate business use of their home.

Common examples include:

Hosting team meetings or planning sessions

Holding client meetings or workshops

Running training sessions or strategy days

Instead of renting a conference room or event space, your business can “rent” your home for those events.

As long as everything is properly documented and the rent is reasonable for your area, this strategy is completely legitimate.

Why This Strategy Is So Powerful

The magic of the Augusta Rule is in how it treats the income.

Normally, rental income is taxable.

But under this rule:

You can receive up to 14 days of rental income

That income is excluded from your personal tax return

You still receive the cash

At the same time:

Your business deducts the rental expense

This can help reduce your overall taxable income

It’s a clean and efficient way to move money from your business to yourself—without the usual tax friction.

How to Make Sure It’s Done Correctly

Like any tax strategy, the Augusta Rule needs to be handled properly.

Here are the key requirements:

The rental must be at fair market value

The rental period must be 14 days or less per year

The activity must be legitimate business use

You should keep proper documentation (invoices, agendas, meeting notes)

One of the most important points: this is not a loophole for personal use.

It must be tied to real business activity, and the documentation should support that.

Why Most Business Owners Miss This

Many business owners simply don’t know this rule exists.

Others:

Don’t document their business use properly

Don’t think their home qualifies

Or assume it’s “too good to be true”

As a result, they miss out on an opportunity to shift income in a completely tax-efficient way.
